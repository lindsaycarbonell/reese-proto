N.C. Congresspeople by the Numbers

April 1, 2014 12:00 AM ET

Analyzing campaign finances in any given election is a task that has been made difficult for decades. Since Theodore Roosevelt's corporate fundraising scandal in 1904, laws have been established to regulate the money flow to candidates for public office. Still, dark money and the rise of super PACs has made tracking the funding candidates receive especially complex.

And while more prominent races — such as those for senators and presidents — are watched more closely, local elections are often left in the dust. This project begins with the U.S. House of Representatives and will progress downwards to district-level elections.

North Carolina has 13 congresspeople in the U.S. House of Representatives. Click on a representative's picture to learn more about the money they received in 2013.

Dark money

Political nonprofits, often called 501 (c) organizations because of the law that protects them, have become a major force in federal elections over the last three cycles. Because they can receive unlimited corporate, individual, or union contributions that they do not have to make public, the term "dark money" is often applied to them. The IRS has done little to enforce limits on political activity, and partly as a result, spending by organizations that do not disclose their donors has increased from less than $5.2 million in 2006 to well over $300 million in the 2012 election. (source: OpenSecrets)

Super PACs

A new type of political action committee (PAC) created after the U.S. Court of Appeals decision in Speechnow v. FEC in 2010. These PACs make no contributions to candidates or parties. They do, however make independent expenditures in federal races - running ads or sending mail or communicating in other ways with messages that specifically advocate the election or defeat of a specific candidate. There are no limits or restrictions on the sources of funds that may be used for these expenditures. These committees file regular financial reports with the FEC which include their donors along with their expenditures. (source: OpenSecrets)
